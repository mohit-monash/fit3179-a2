Australian Affordability

How quickly have housing costs outrun incomes across Australia? We combine tax, wage and price indicators to track the purchasing power of households nationally, across capital cities and down to SA3 communities.

National overview: buying a home takes more years of income

The price-to-income ratio compares the mean dwelling price in each state and territory to the average taxable income earned by residents in the same year. Ratios above the national benchmark mean buyers require more years of income than the Australian average.

Key takeaways, sources and caveats

What we learned

  • Sydney and Melbourne remain the least affordable markets, with a typical dwelling costing 18–19 years of taxable income versus 14 nationally.
  • Within every capital city there are pockets where the ratio exceeds 22, even when using state-level dwelling prices— indicating severe income shortfalls relative to housing costs.
  • Wage growth has lagged both CPI and property price growth since 2011, eroding real purchasing power despite recent income gains.

Methodology & sources

Incomes from the ATO individual tax dataset (financial years 2017–18 to 2021–22) are aggregated to SA3, state and national levels. Property prices come from the ABS mean dwelling price series (quarterly, aggregated here to the June quarter for each year). CPI and average weekly earnings are ABS capital city series. Price-to-income ratios divide mean dwelling price (dollar value) by average taxable income for the same geography and year. Salary & wage share is the proportion of tax filers receiving wage income.

Geographic boundaries: ASGS 2021 SA3 and GCCSA regions. Time axis: financial years end in June. Ratios rely on state-level property prices; intra-city variation reflects differences in income only.
