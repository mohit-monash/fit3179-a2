Australian Affordability

Housing affordability has tightened nationwide as dwelling prices stretch further away from household incomes. This dashboard traces the price–to–income ratio from long-run context to local labour markets to show where pressure is most acute.

Long-run context

Indexing prices, wages and inflation to 2011 reveals how property values have accelerated faster than earnings and the consumer price index throughout the 2010s and early 2020s.

Why this matters

  • Dwelling prices almost double (index ≈210) by 2025, outstripping wages near 150 and CPI near 140.
  • The widening gap sets the baseline for price–to–income ratios in later sections.

How to read

The indexed lines use 2011 = 100 so changes show relative growth rather than absolute price levels.

Hover to inspect specific financial years and note the turning points that align with policy or economic shocks.

National snapshot

The scoreboard summarises four affordability markers for the selected area. These controls affect the cards only, letting you compare cities independently of the other charts.

Key signals

  • Price–to–income ratios exceed 13 years nationally in 2021–22, with Sydney extending beyond 14 years.
  • Mean dwelling values sit roughly 6× average taxable income, underscoring the reliance on credit and accumulated wealth.

How to read

Cards update instantly as you switch the city selector. Ratios use financial-year tax data to align incomes with price benchmarks.

Salary share captures the proportion of income drawn from wages, signalling sensitivity to labour market shocks.

Distribution of price–to–income ratios

Histograms show how affordability varies across Statistical Area Level 3 (SA3) regions. Each panel normalises counts to highlight the spread within and across states.

What stands out

  • By 2021–22 most NSW and Victorian SA3s cluster above 12 years, while Western Australia retains a broader spread below 10.
  • Median ratios (purple rules) lag the mean, showing high-price outliers stretching the distribution tails.

How to read

Each bar shows the share of SA3s in a half-year band. Orange and purple rules mark the mean and median respectively.

Use this chart’s year dropdown to explore different periods independently.

Capital city comparison

Average taxable incomes (●) and mean dwelling prices (▲) are plotted for each capital. Labels show the resulting price–to–income ratio in years.

Why this matters

  • Sydney remains the least affordable capital with ratios beyond 14 years even after the pandemic peak.
  • Darwin and Perth cluster nearer to 9 years, reflecting stronger income growth relative to prices.

How to read

The line between the ● and ▲ markers indicates the wedge between income and price. Shorter lines signal more manageable ratios.

Use the national region selector above to align notes with a specific capital.

States and territories over time

Tracking price–to–income ratios from 2018–2022 shows how each jurisdiction has diverged. The shared palette helps match state colours across charts.

Key movements

  • New South Wales and Victoria remain in the high band above 12 years across the period.
  • Queensland and South Australia accelerate sharply post-2020, mirroring internal migration flows.

Mapping SA3 price–to–income ratios highlights the geographic skew within selected capitals. The ranking table alongside shows where incomes are most stretched.

Local insight

  • Inner-city and coastal SA3s register the highest ratios, signalling intense competition for limited stock.
  • Outer suburban zones remain below 10 years yet face slower income growth, constraining future resilience.

Income mix and resilience

The share of income derived from wages and salaries signals how exposed households are to shifts in the labour market when servicing housing costs.

Why this matters

  • States with higher wage reliance, such as the Northern Territory, are more sensitive to employment cycles.
  • South Australia’s lower wage share aligns with slower income growth, compounding affordability strain.

How to read

Bars show the proportion of taxable income sourced from wages. Compare with price–to–income levels to assess resilience.

Use this section’s year dropdown to align the salary mix with other views when needed.

Key findings and sources

Headline messages

  • Price–to–income ratios remain above 12 years in NSW and Victoria, with Sydney still topping 14 years.
  • Western Australia and the Northern Territory stay closer to 9 years thanks to stronger wage growth.
  • Income reliance on wages leaves many households exposed to labour market shocks when rates rise.

Sources & method

Data: Australian Taxation Office individual tax statistics (2017–18 to 2021–22); Australian Bureau of Statistics Mean Dwelling Price (cat. 6416.0) and Average Weekly Earnings (cat. 6302.0); ABS Consumer Price Index capital city series (cat. 6401.0).

Method: Price–to–income ratio divides mean dwelling price by average taxable income for matching regions. Salary share reflects wages as a share of taxable income. Indexed series use 2011 = 100. All values are in nominal dollars.

Author: Mohit Singh (33143536). Caveats: Tax data uses individuals lodging returns and may understate informal income; property prices use mean values that are sensitive to high-end transactions.
